02 / Research

The bench-verified property and the deployed behaviour are different things.

A model that scores well on a static benchmark is not the same as a system that behaves trustworthily once a person, a workflow, and a deployment context wrap around it. My work treats alignment as ongoing maintenance rather than a one-time manufacturing step, and builds the rubrics, instruments, and interfaces that maintenance requires. The active threads: deployment-time alignment, interactional benchmarks, and the structural limits of dual-use ethics.

03 / Flagship

The Virtuous Intelligence Framework.

A deployment-time alignment framework operationalising three mechanisms - derived from grounded-theory analysis of human–agent interaction breakdowns. Currently at v3.0; a Live VIF Monitor is wired into a frontier LLM API for controlled validation.

01

Scoping

Was the request bounded? Did the system, the user, or the interface negotiate what's in and what's out before the model went to work?

Operationalised as
  • Pre-task contracts (explicit constraints & success criteria)
  • Negotiation moves visible in transcript
  • Out-of-scope detection & refusal coherence
02

Signalling

Did the model communicate confidence, uncertainty, and limits? Did it surface enough of its reasoning that the user could verify rather than just trust?

Operationalised as
  • Calibrated uncertainty markers
  • Evidence-pointing & verification scaffolds
  • Honest disclosure of capability boundaries
03

Repair

When the user pushed back, did the model recover? Did it help the user update what they wanted when their initial ask was wrong - or did it just agree?

Operationalised as
  • Grounding restoration after breakdown
  • Sycophancy resistance under social pressure
  • Re-scoping moves as conversation evolves

05 / Writing

Notes & half-formed arguments.

Topics span alignment, AI governance, philosophy of AI, human-centered AI, and the occasional methodological gripe.

2 May 2026 Alignment · VIF · Maintenance 3 min

Alignment is a verb.

The most consequential framing error in alignment research is grammatical. We talk about an aligned model the way we talk about a calibrated thermometer, as if alignment were a property the artefact possesses, fixed at manufacture, verifiable on the bench. It isn't.

Three things follow if you take this seriously. First, a model can pass every pre-release benchmark and still behave badly in deployment, because deployment changes the system: the user, the workflow, the prior turns of conversation, the integrations. None of these are present at training time. Second, the tools we have are calibrated for the wrong layer. Most alignment benchmarks score the model in isolation; almost none score whether the model-plus-context behaves trustworthily. The 16-benchmark audit my collaborators and I ran shows this directly: of eight interactional dimensions, verification support gets a non-zero score on zero benchmarks. Third, and this is the part that matters: if alignment is maintenance work, then the field needs a vocabulary for that work. Not just training-time mitigations and post-hoc evaluations, but instruments for the messy middle, the part where humans and AI systems are actually collaborating. That vocabulary doesn't yet exist. Building it is what my thesis is about.

22 Apr 2026 Governance · Dual-Use · Agency 2 min

Where does responsibility actually live in AI research?

The standard story about dual-use research goes: a researcher invents something, considers whether it could be misused, and either publishes responsibly or doesn't. It's a bad story. Awareness isn't the binding constraint; agency is.

By the time a model is in the hands of a downstream developer, who then ships it to a startup, who then integrates it into a product used by a million people, the original researcher has lost the ability to meaningfully alter the trajectory. The locus of responsibility has moved even though the harm got bigger. Most ethics frameworks point at the researcher, the place where epistemic distance from the harm is greatest and agency is lowest. That's the structural mistake. The work I'm doing with Marina Jirotka asks the inverse question: where in the pipeline is epistemic distance shortest and agency highest? Govern there. The answer almost never points back at the original researcher. It points at the developer integrating the model, the platform hosting it, the deployer configuring it for a specific population. We owe each of these actors a different theory of responsibility.

20 Mar 2026 Evaluation · Benchmarks · Audit 2 min

What we found auditing 16 alignment benchmarks.

Inter-rater κ = 0.87, eight dimensions, one uncomfortable conclusion: deployment-relevant alignment cannot be inferred from model-level audits. Verification support is absent across the ecosystem; process steerability is nearly absent. The four benchmarks plausibly intended as interactional spike on different dimensions; no two share a coverage profile. WildBench draws on more than a million real user logs and still scores zero on five of eight dimensions, because the construction of the evaluation, not the source of the data, determines what gets measured. The audit is now a public repository: rubric, ledger, analysis code, all the disagreement-resolution rationale. If you want to replicate it, extend it, or argue with it, the artefacts are there. That's the point. Reproducibility for evaluation work isn't a virtue; it's a precondition for the work being scientific at all.
